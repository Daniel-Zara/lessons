Assassins and Templars: The History of Secret Orders, Influence, and Legacy

Introduction

Throughout history, secret societies, mysterious brotherhoods, and military orders have shaped the political and military landscape of their time. Among them, the Assassins and Templars stand out as two legendary factions whose rivalry influenced medieval history and continues to captivate modern audiences.

The Assassins were masters of stealth and subterfuge, eliminating powerful figures through carefully planned assassinations. The Templars, on the other hand, were an elite military order with immense wealth, power, and influence over European politics and finance. Their ideological and strategic clash has become a central theme in modern fiction, especially in the Assassin’s Creed series.

But who were the real Assassins and Templars? How did their paths cross, and what secrets does history hide about them? Let’s explore their origins, tactics, and ultimate fate.

The Real Assassins: Shadows of the Middle Ages

Origins and Principles of the Order

The Order of Assassins emerged in the late 11th century in Persia and Syria. Its founder, Hassan-i Sabbah, was a brilliant strategist and religious leader who created a network of devoted followers. They were members of the Nizari Ismaili sect of Islam and saw themselves as warriors fighting against their enemies, including the Seljuk Empire and the Crusaders.

Rather than engaging in open warfare, the Assassins specialized in targeted killings of key political and military figures. Their name is believed to come from the Arabic word ḥašīšiyyīn, which some claim means "hashish users," although the true meaning remains debated.

Tactics and Methods

The Assassins' success lay in their highly sophisticated tactics:

  • Disguise and infiltration – They lived among their enemies, sometimes for years, before striking.
  • Careful planning – Every assassination was meticulously prepared, considering the habits and routines of the target.
  • Precision over chaos – They killed only specific individuals rather than waging large-scale battles.
  • Hidden weapons – Daggers, poison, and small concealed blades were their tools of choice.

Their most famous stronghold was Alamut Castle, located in present-day Iran. It served as a training center where initiates learned philosophy, martial arts, and strategic warfare.

The Fall of the Order

Despite their cunning tactics, the Assassins could not withstand the overwhelming force of the Mongols. In 1256, Hulagu Khan, the grandson of Genghis Khan, led a devastating campaign against them, destroying Alamut and other Assassin fortresses. Although remnants of the Order continued to operate for a while, their influence eventually faded.

The Knights Templar: Guardians of the Holy Land or Masters of Manipulation?

The Birth of the Order

The Knights Templar, officially known as the Poor Fellow-Soldiers of Christ and of the Temple of Solomon, were founded in 1119 in Jerusalem. Their initial mission was to protect Christian pilgrims traveling to the Holy Land. However, over time, the Templars evolved into one of the most powerful military and financial organizations in medieval Europe.

Templar knights wore distinctive white cloaks with a red cross and were renowned for their discipline, martial skills, and unwavering faith.

Wealth and Influence

The Templars became immensely wealthy and powerful through various means:

  • Financial services – They established an early banking system, allowing travelers to deposit money in one location and withdraw it elsewhere.
  • Land ownership – They controlled vast estates across Europe, from France to England and Spain.
  • Political influence – They advised and even financed kings, shaping the fate of kingdoms.

Downfall: Betrayal and Suppression

Their growing power made them enemies, particularly King Philip IV of France, who owed them massive debts. On Friday, October 13, 1307, he orchestrated the arrest of the Templars, accusing them of heresy, devil worship, and corrupt practices. Under torture, many confessed to fabricated crimes, leading to the order's downfall.

In 1312, Pope Clement V officially dissolved the Knights Templar. Their last Grand Master, Jacques de Molay, was burned at the stake in 1314. Before his execution, he reportedly cursed King Philip and Pope Clement, both of whom died within a year, fueling legends of the Templars' vengeance.

Some believe that surviving Templars fled to Scotland or Portugal, where they continued their activities under different names.

Assassins vs. Templars: A Historical Feud or Fictional Rivalry?

Although there is little evidence that Assassins and Templars frequently clashed in real history, their rivalry has become legendary, especially in popular culture.

In the Assassin’s Creed series, the Assassins are portrayed as fighters for freedom, opposing the Templars, who seek absolute control over humanity. However, both historical orders had striking similarities:

  • Strict hierarchy and discipline – Both groups followed a rigid code of conduct.
  • Secrecy and intelligence networks – Each order maintained a web of spies and informants.
  • Ideological warfare – They saw themselves as upholding a higher purpose.

The main difference? The Assassins relied on precision and subterfuge, while the Templars wielded military and economic power.

The Legacy of the Assassins and Templars

Today, the legends of these secretive orders continue to inspire conspiracy theories, historical research, and entertainment.

  • Books and Movies – The Templars feature in The Da Vinci Code and National Treasure, while the Assassins remain a subject of historical fascination.
  • Video Games – Assassin’s Creed introduced millions to the Assassin-Templar conflict.
  • Modern Conspiracy Theories – Some claim the Freemasons are descendants of the Templars, while others believe the Assassins’ teachings live on in covert military operations.

Conclusion

The Assassins and Templars left an indelible mark on history and culture. Their struggle was not just about power but about ideology—one side valuing secrecy and precision, the other striving for dominance and control.

But who was truly right? Were the Assassins freedom fighters or cold-blooded killers? Were the Templars noble warriors or ruthless schemers?
